# 🛡️ FakeGuard — AI Fake Review Detector

An NLP-based system that detects AI-generated (fake) product reviews using text classification, deployed as an interactive web app with prediction explanations.

🔗 **Live Demo:** https://fakeguard-ai-review-detector-3fwxmvdjs4nrvlwzpzbxye.streamlit.app/
📂 **Repo:** https://github.com/Pritamrajput1410/FakeGuard-AI-Review-Detector

![App Screenshot](docs/app_screenshot.png)

 ## 🎯 Problem

Fake reviews are a widespread, well-documented problem across e-commerce platforms like Amazon, Yelp, and TripAdvisor. They mislead customers into poor purchasing decisions, unfairly inflate or damage product/seller reputations, and erode trust in online review systems generally. Detecting them is an active area of investment for major platforms — this project explores whether classical NLP and machine learning techniques can reliably distinguish AI-generated reviews from genuine ones, and — just as importantly — *why* they succeed or fail at it.


## 🧠 Approach

1. **Data:** 40,432 labeled product reviews ([Kaggle Fake Reviews Dataset](https://www.kaggle.com/)) — perfectly balanced between Computer-Generated (CG) and Original (OR) labels, spanning multiple product categories.
2. **Preprocessing:** Lowercasing, punctuation removal, stopword removal, and lemmatization using NLTK.
3. **Feature Engineering:** Beyond raw text, engineered additional signals:
   - Review length
   - Sentiment polarity (via TextBlob)
   - **Sentiment–rating mismatch** — a custom feature measuring disagreement between a review's star rating and its actual text sentiment
   - Exclamation mark and superlative word counts
4. **Text Representation — compared two approaches:**
   - **TF-IDF** (word/phrase frequency-based, with unigrams + bigrams)
   - **Sentence-BERT** (`all-MiniLM-L6-v2`) — pretrained transformer embeddings capturing semantic meaning
5. **Models:** Multinomial Naive Bayes and Logistic Regression, trained and evaluated across both representations.
6. **Interpretability:** Extracted top predictive words per class from model coefficients, and built a per-review explanation function showing exactly which words drove each individual prediction.
7. **Deployment:** Packaged into reusable Python modules (`src/`) and deployed as a live Streamlit web app.


## 📊 Results
| Representation | Model                   | Accuracy | F1 (macro) |
|---             |---                      |---       |---         |
| TF-IDF         | Naive Bayes             | 0.86     | 0.86       |
| **TF-IDF**     | **Logistic Regression** | **0.89** | **0.89**   |
| Sentence-BERT  | Logistic Regression     | 0.81     | 0.81       |




## 💡 Key Insights

**TF-IDF outperformed Sentence-BERT embeddings**, despite the latter being the more "modern" technique. The likely reason: this task depends on *surface-level lexical and stylistic patterns* — specific word choices and phrasing quirks — rather than *semantic meaning*. A real and a fake review of the same product are typically about the same subject matter, so a representation built to capture topic/meaning (Sentence-BERT) has less to distinguish between them. TF-IDF, built specifically around word-frequency patterns, is better suited to catching these stylistic "tells."

**The most predictive words for genuine reviews were hedging and qualifying language** — words like *"even," "though," "actually," "however,"* and *"without."* This suggests real reviews tend to contain more nuanced, qualified language reflecting authentic, imperfect experiences (e.g., "it's good, though the color was slightly off"), while AI-generated reviews tend toward more uniform, unqualified statements. This was a genuinely unexpected finding — I initially assumed "gushing/superlative-heavy" language would be the strongest fake signal, but the data showed the opposite: several superlative words (*"absolutely," "highly," "ever"*) actually leaned toward predicting **real**, not fake.

**Before trusting these results, I explicitly checked for label leakage** — verifying that fake and real reviews were evenly distributed across all star ratings (1-5), not concentrated in certain ratings. This confirmed the model was learning genuine language patterns rather than exploiting a shortcut like "5-star reviews are usually fake."

## ⚠️ Limitations

- The dataset consists of AI-generated fake reviews created for research purposes — not reviews caught being fraudulent on a live platform. Real-world fake reviews (written by humans to deceive, or generated by different/newer LLMs) may follow different patterns than this model has learned.
- TF-IDF-based interpretability, while useful, can sometimes surface words that are statistically correlated but not intuitively meaningful — word importance should be read as a pattern signal, not a definitive explanation.
- The model is trained on English-language reviews from specific product categories; performance on other domains or languages is untested.
